## SECCIÓN 1: HOOK DE APERTURA — El Momento de Terror

It was Thanksgiving dinner when it happened.

My granddaughter Emma ran up to me, arms wide open, that big four-year-old smile on her face. And I froze.

**I couldn't remember her name.**

Not for one second. Not for two. For what felt like an eternity, I stood there — in my own daughter's kitchen, surrounded by my own family — and I could not pull my granddaughter's name out of my brain.

*"Grandma? Are you okay?"*

It was my daughter Lauren who said it. And when I looked around the table, I saw it. The look. That quiet, careful look that people give you when they're worried but don't want to say it out loud.

My husband David reached under the table and squeezed my hand. He didn't say anything.

He didn't have to.

**That moment changed everything.** Not because it was the first time I'd forgotten something. I'd been forgetting things for months — names, words, where I put my reading glasses, whether I'd already told a story.

But it was the first time I saw **fear** in my family's eyes.

And it was the first time I admitted to myself what I'd been terrified of for years:

*It's happening to me. The same thing that happened to my mother.*

My mother, Eleanor, was the sharpest woman I ever knew. A high school English teacher for 35 years. She could quote Shakespeare, finish the Sunday crossword in pen, and remember every student's name — even decades later.

She was diagnosed with Alzheimer's at 68.

It started small. Forgetting a neighbor's name. Losing her car in the parking lot. Asking the same question three times in one conversation.

By 72, she didn't know what day it was.

By 75, **she didn't know who I was.**

I spent seven years watching my mother disappear — not all at once, but piece by piece, word by word, memory by memory. Until the woman who raised me, who taught me to love books and language and learning, looked at me with completely empty eyes and said:

*"Are you one of the nurses?"*

**Am I next?**

What I discovered over the following three months didn't just change my life — it changed everything I thought I knew about memory loss, brain aging, and whether any of it could actually be stopped.

## SECCIÓN 2: EL DOLOR — El Problema Profundo

But it wasn't just forgetfulness. It was something deeper.

**My brain felt slower.** Like thinking through fog. Like there was a half-second delay between wanting to say something and actually finding the words.

That gentle voice. That's what killed me.

It was the same voice I used with my mother when she started repeating herself. Patient. Careful. **Scared.**

Normal. Expected. Try crossword puzzles.
